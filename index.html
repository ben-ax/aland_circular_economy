Sustainability Agenda

Åland's circular economy work is built into the Development and Sustainability Agenda (bärkraft.ax), with a target of full sustainability by 2051. Key 2030 goals include reducing waste to 1.2 tonnes per person per year and having 80% of the population living by circular principles. Åland won the European Sustainability Award in 2019 for public entities under 100,000 inhabitants.

The construction sector produces a massive amount of waste. The Waste to Value project (2025–2028) is a standout example of how Åland is rethinking building materials.

  • EU-Funded Initiative: This project is funded by the Central Baltic programme to systematically reduce the use of new materials.
  • The Jomala Hub: Led by Emmaus Åland, this reuse facility lets businesses and individuals drop off building materials instead of sending them to incineration.
  • Baltic Sea Impact: Policy recommendations from the project will be shared to influence circular practices across the entire Baltic Sea region.

The hotel sector in Åland is taking strong steps toward sustainability through official eco-certifications and creative low-waste approaches.

  • Eco-Certifications: Hotell Pommern carries the Nordic Swan ecolabel, while Hotel Arkipelag and Park Alandia Hotell hold the Green Key.
  • Skipped Cleanings: Ålandhotels donates €1 to the Keep the Archipelago Tidy Association when guests skip daily cleaning, generating over €10,000 annually.
  • Heritage Reuse: Hotel Svala in Kumlinge is a converted 1930s hospital focusing on seasonal organic food and a low-waste ethos.

In a linear economy, used products are treated as waste. In Åland's circular economy, waste management focuses on preparing used items to become raw materials for new products.

  • Massive Volume: Emmaus Åland handles around 450,000 kg of donated goods per year.
  • Local Resale: 62% of these donated goods are sold locally right back into the community.
  • Repair Workshops: They run hands-on repair and reuse workshops for clothing and furniture.
  • Efficient Design: To make the circular economy work, products must be designed so they can easily be reused as raw materials after their lifespan ends.
